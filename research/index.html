Research Interests:
  • Structure of memory:
    I am interested in understanding how our brain maps sensory input to a neuronal network. The brain needs to create structured memory modules in order to utilize this information and produce an appropriate response to the sensory stimuli in return.
    I suspect current deep-learning paradigm might fall short of being able to replicate such memory modules in all its complexity. Deep-learning paradigm uses unstructured sets of active neurons that represent a very weak data structure. The nature of brute-force training through thousands of examples only results in fine-tuned connectomes that, perhaps, are weak in feature representation. There are few methods developed to increase robustness in network predictions by random deactivation of neurons while training; however it does not get us as far. We need to implement causally top-down approach (e.g. feature binding) in order to constrain such networks thereby increasing the information content in the system.
    I think making advancements in establishing richer memory modules inspired by biological brains would accelerate the field of artificial intelligence.

  • Autonomous agents in embedded systems:
    I am intersted in constructing artificial-neural-network based agents (a Breitenberg vehicle embedded in an environment) that are governed by self-organization principles. It is interesting to figure out how to bring about a consistent integration of top-down contextuality (agent-environment interaction) and bottom-up organizing principles (how an agent dynamically organizes itself in order to meet the challenges introduced by a changing environment) for an autonomous agents.

  • Nature of Gravity:
    I spend a lot of time thinking about the nature of gravitational interaction and nature of spacetime. During my masters thesis I tried to understand the notion of emergent phenomena in Physics. Since gravitational field equations have same conceptual status as equations of fluid mechanics, how much can we learn about nature of gravitational interaction if it is an emergent phenomena like fluid mechanics?
    What is the essential role of quantum mechanics in explaining gravitational interaction? Is the world fundamentally quantum mechanical? Is gravity, same as hydrodynamics, manifestly classical? If so, such a quantum theory of gravity is very different from theory of quantized gravity (One can think of theory of quantized gravity would be similar to phonon-physics.). These are few of the questions that keep me up at night.
    I think, in order to find a conmplete theory of quantum gravity, we need to pay attention to three main theoretical constructs, viz.,
    • Black-hole singularity
    • Big-bang singularity
    • Cosmological constant problem
    about which both gravitational and quantum theory has something to say. Any theory of quantum gravity must find a resolution to the aforementioned constructs.
